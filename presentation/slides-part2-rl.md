# 2.1. Part II — Reinforcement learning extension

## From implicit OC to learned policies with unknown dynamics

- We keep the **value-function parameterization** $\phi_\theta(t,z)$.
- The control is still defined implicitly through the Hamiltonian stationarity condition.
- New difficulty: the dynamics $f$ are **unknown**.
- We only observe transitions from rollouts.

$$
\text{Unknown: } f,\ \nabla_z f,\ \nabla_u f
\qquad
\text{Known: } L,\ G,\ \rho
$$

---

## 2.2. From continuous OC to discrete rollouts

Continuous-time dynamics:

$$
\dot z(t)=f(t,z(t),u(t)), \qquad z(0)=x.
$$

Discretize on $t_k=k\Delta t$, $k=0,\dots,N$, $T=N\Delta t$:

$$
z_{k+1}=F(t_k,z_k,u_k),
$$

with the one-step transition map

$$
F(t,z,u) =
z+\Delta t\, f(t,z,u)+O(\Delta t^2).
$$

The empirical RL setting gives access to samples

$$
(z_k,u_k,z_{k+1}),
$$

but not to the analytical formula of $F$.

---

## 2.3. Discrete objective

For one initial condition $x$, the discretized objective is

$$
J_x(\theta) =
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}
L(t_k,z_k,u^\star_{\theta,k})\,\Delta t
+
G(z_N).
$$

The training objective is therefore

$$
\min_\theta \ \mathbb E_{x\sim \rho} J_x(\theta).
$$

- Same optimal-control objective as before.
- But the trajectory is now generated by interacting with the environment.

---

## 2.4. Local Jacobian estimation: target

At each training iteration, we roll out the current implicit policy and obtain

$$
\bar z_{0:N},
\qquad
\bar u_{0:N-1}.
$$

We estimate local Jacobians of the unknown transition map:

$$
A_k \approx \nabla_z F(t_k,\bar z_k,\bar u_k),
\qquad
B_k \approx \nabla_u F(t_k,\bar z_k,\bar u_k).
$$

These replace the missing analytical derivatives of the dynamics.

---

## 2.5. On-trajectory Jacobian estimation

Around the reference trajectory, perturb the controls:

$$ u^{(i)}_{0:N-1} = \bar u_{0:N-1} + \delta u^{(i)}, \qquad \delta u^{(i)}\sim \mathcal N(0,\sigma^2 I).$$


The first-order expansion gives

$$
\Delta z^{(i)}_{k+1}
\approx
A_k^\top \Delta z^{(i)}_k
+
B_k^\top \Delta u^{(i)}_k,
$$

with

$$
\Delta z^{(i)}_k=z^{(i)}_k-\bar z_k,
\qquad
\Delta u^{(i)}_k=u^{(i)}_k-\bar u_k.
$$

Estimate $A_k,B_k$ by least squares:

$$
(A_k,B_k)= \arg\min_{A,B} \sum_{i=1}^M \left\| A^\top \Delta z^{(i)}_k + B^\top \Delta u^{(i)}_k -
\Delta z^{(i)}_{k+1}
\right\|^2.
$$

---

## 2.6. Off-trajectory / RLS Jacobian estimation

A more sample-efficient alternative is recursive least squares.

Use the augmented regressor

$$
\zeta_t^{(k)} =
\begin{bmatrix}
x_t^{(k)}\\
u_t^{(k)}\\
1
\end{bmatrix},
$$

and update

$$
Q_t^{(k)} =
\alpha_{\mathrm{rls}} Q_t^{(k-1)}
+
(1-\alpha_{\mathrm{rls}})q_0 I
+
\zeta_t^{(k)}(\zeta_t^{(k)})^\top,
$$

$$
F_t^{(k)} = F_t^{(k-1)} + \left(Q_t^{(k)}\right)^{-1} \zeta_t^{(k)} \left(
x_{t+1}^{(k)} -
F_t^{(k-1)}\zeta_t^{(k)}
\right)^\top.
$$

where

$$
F_t=[A_t^\top \ B_t^\top \ c_t].
$$

- $t$ = time index inside a trajectory.
- $k$ = rollout / update index.
- Works best when successive rollouts remain locally informative.

---

## 2.7. Local linear transition model

Both estimation methods produce a local model

$$
\widehat F_k(z,u) =
\bar z_{k+1}
+
A_k^\top(z-\bar z_k)
+
B_k^\top(u-\bar u_k).
$$

It is valid only near the current trajectory point

$$
(\bar z_k,\bar u_k).
$$

This is the key replacement:

$$
\text{unknown } F
\quad \leadsto \quad
\text{local estimated linearization } \widehat F_k.
$$

---

## 2.8. Estimated Hamiltonian

Define the estimated continuous-time vector field

$$
\widehat f_k(z,u) =
A_k^\top(z-\bar z_k)
+
B_k^\top(u-\bar u_k)
+
\frac{\bar z_{k+1}-\bar z_k}{\Delta t}.
$$

Then the estimated Hamiltonian is

$$
\widehat H_k(z,p,u) = -\langle p,\widehat f_k(z,u)\rangle -
L(t_k,z,u).
$$

The fixed-point operator becomes

$$
\widehat T_{\theta,k}(u;z) =
u+\alpha
\nabla_u \widehat H_k
\bigl(
z,\nabla_z\phi_\theta(t_k,z),u
\bigr).
$$

---

## 2.9. Key simplification

The control-gradient of the estimated Hamiltonian is

$$
\nabla_u \widehat H_k(z,p,u) = -B_k^\top p -
\nabla_u L(t_k,z,u).
$$

Therefore the implicit policy can be computed without evaluating $f$:

$$
u^\star_{\theta,k} =
\widehat T_{\theta,k}
(
u^\star_{\theta,k};z_k
).
$$

The only dynamics information needed inside the fixed-point operator is

$$
B_k \approx \nabla_u F.
$$

---

## 2.10. Discrete adjoint with estimated dynamics

The data-driven adjoint is computed backward:

$$
p_N=\nabla G(\bar z_N),
$$

$$
p_k =
A_k^\top p_{k+1}
+
\Delta t\,\nabla_z L(t_k,\bar z_k,\bar u_k),
\qquad
k=N-1,\dots,0.
$$

Interpretation:

$$
A_k^\top p_{k+1} =
\text{future sensitivity propagated through the dynamics},
$$

$$
\Delta t\,\nabla_z L =
\text{instantaneous state-cost sensitivity}.
$$

---

## 2.11. Two costates in the pipeline

Inside the fixed-point operator, we use the value-gradient costate:

$$
p_\theta(t_k,z_k) =
\nabla_z \phi_\theta(t_k,z_k).
$$

For the gradient computation, we also use the trajectory-based costate:

$$
\widehat p_k =
A_k^\top \widehat p_{k+1}
+
\Delta t\,\nabla_z L(t_k,\bar z_k,\bar u_k).
$$

So:

| Role | Costate used |
|---|---|
| Compute implicit control | $\nabla_z\phi_\theta(t_k,z_k)$ |
| Compute descent direction | $\widehat p_k$ from backward adjoint recursion |

---

## 2.12. JFB gradient with estimated dynamics

The exact gradient has the structure

$$
\frac{dJ_x}{d\theta} =
\int_0^T
\left(
\frac{d u^\star_\theta}{d\theta}(t,\bar z_t)
\right)^\top
\left[
\nabla_u L(t,\bar z_t,u^\star_\theta)
+
(\nabla_u f)^\top p_t
\right]dt.
$$

But both terms are problematic:

$$
\nabla_u f \text{ unknown},
\qquad
\frac{d u^\star_\theta}{d\theta} =
\left(
I-\frac{\partial T_\theta}{\partial u}
\right)^{-1}
\frac{\partial T_\theta}{\partial\theta}
\text{ expensive}.
$$

---

## 2.13. Track A gradient actually used

We replace

$$
\nabla_u f
\quad \leadsto \quad
B_k,
$$

and apply JFB:

$$
\frac{d u^\star_\theta}{d\theta}
\quad \leadsto \quad
\frac{\partial \widehat T_{\theta,k}}{\partial\theta}.
$$

Therefore the estimated JFB descent direction is

$$
\widehat{\frac{dJ_x}{d\theta}} =
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}
\left(
\frac{\partial \widehat T_{\theta,k}}{\partial\theta}
\right)^\top
\left[
\nabla_u L(t_k,\bar z_k,u^\star_{\theta,k})
+
B_k^\top \widehat p_{k+1}
\right]
\Delta t.
$$

This is the central training formula of the RL extension.

---

## 2.14. Algorithmic summary

For each epoch:

1. Sample initial condition

$$
x\sim \rho.
$$

2. Forward rollout in the environment:

$$
z_{k+1}=F(t_k,z_k,u_k).
$$

3. Compute controls by the implicit fixed point:

$$
u_k =
\widehat T_{\theta,k}(u_k;z_k).
$$

4. Update Jacobian estimates:

$$
A_k,\ B_k.
$$

5. Backward adjoint pass:

$$
p_k=A_k^\top p_{k+1}+\Delta t\,\nabla_z L.
$$

6. JFB update:

$$
\theta
\leftarrow
\theta -
\eta
\widehat{\frac{dJ_x}{d\theta}}.
$$

---

## 2.15. Sources of error

The estimated gradient differs from the true gradient through three terms:

| Error source | Where it enters |
|---|---|
| $A_k-\nabla_z F_k$ | backward adjoint recursion |
| $B_k-\nabla_u F_k$ | Hamiltonian gradient and JFB bracket |
| JFB approximation | replaces full implicit derivative |

Full derivative:

$$
\left(
I-\frac{\partial T_\theta}{\partial u}
\right)^{-1}
\frac{\partial T_\theta}{\partial\theta}
$$

JFB approximation:

$$
\frac{\partial T_\theta}{\partial\theta}.
$$

## 2.16. Van der Pol oscillator — control problem

### Goal

Learn a feedback control that **stabilizes** a nonlinear oscillator and drives the state toward

$$
z_{\text{target}} = (0,0).
$$

The state is

$$
z(t)=
\begin{pmatrix}
x_1(t)\\
x_2(t)
\end{pmatrix},
$$

where:

- $x_1$ = position-like variable  
- $x_2$ = velocity-like variable  

### Dynamics

The controlled Van der Pol system is

$$
\dot x_1 = x_2,
$$

$$
\dot x_2 = \mu(1-x_1^2)x_2 - x_1 + u.
$$

- The term $\mu(1-x_1^2)x_2 - x_1$ is the **natural nonlinear dynamics**
- The control $u(t)$ acts like an **external force**

### What is our control?

$$
u(t)\in\mathbb R
$$

It is a scalar control input chosen at each time step to counteract the natural oscillations and steer the system to the origin.


---

## 2.17. Portfolio optimal control — control problem

### Goal

Learn an investment policy that chooses how much wealth to allocate to a risky asset over time, while balancing:

- **growth of wealth**
- **risk / concentration penalty**

The state is the wealth

$$
W_t \in \mathbb R.
$$

### Dynamics

The wealth evolves according to

$$
\dot W = rW + \pi(\mu-r)W.
$$

- $\mu$ = drift of the risky asset  
- $r$ = risk-free rate  
- both are treated as **unknown** in the RL setting

### Running and terminal costs

We minimize

$$
\int_0^T L(t,W,\pi)\,dt + G(W_T),
$$

with

$$
L(t,W,\pi)=\lambda\left(e^{\pi^2}-1\right),
\qquad
G(W)=-\log\left(\frac{W}{W_{\mathrm{ref}}}\right).
$$

So the agent seeks high terminal wealth while penalizing overly aggressive positions.

## 2.23. Practical challenges

- **Locality of RLS:** old rollouts may be misleading if they are far from the current trajectory.
- **Jacobian warm-up:** early $A_k,B_k$ estimates may be noisy.
- **Contractivity:** the fixed-point operator must remain stable.
- **Long-horizon sensitivity:** errors in $A_k$ propagate backward through the adjoint recursion.
- **Exploration:** enough perturbation is needed to identify $B_k$.

Diagnostics:

$$
\|z_{k+1}-\widehat F_k(z_k,u_k)\|,
$$

$$
\partial_t\phi_\theta+\sup_u \widehat H_k,
$$

and fixed-point residuals.

---

## 2.24. Outlook

The extension keeps the Gelphman structure:

$$
\phi_\theta
\quad\rightarrow\quad
\nabla_z\phi_\theta
\quad\rightarrow\quad
u^\star_\theta
\quad\rightarrow\quad
J(\theta).
$$

But replaces analytical dynamics by estimated local Jacobians:

$$
f,\nabla f
\quad\leadsto\quad
A_k,B_k.
$$

Next steps:

- unknown $L$ via cost-gradient estimation;
- stochastic dynamics and second-order HJB terms;
- stronger proof of descent under simultaneous JFB and Jacobian-estimation error;
- multi-asset portfolio benchmarks.
